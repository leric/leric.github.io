## A tiny sketch of the claim

When attempted change is cheap, the bottleneck stops being “typing speed” and becomes the cost of acquiring enough context to modify the system correctly. CMP calls that burden **context cost**: the cost of acquiring sufficient context for correct modification.[^load]

If agents widen the funnel of attempted edits without making required context cheaper to acquire, failures migrate from syntax errors to *semantic* and *systemic* ones. That is not an argument against agents; it is an argument for a **theory of software design** that treats context cost as a first-class design concern.

## Math as notation (not a claim of precision)

Let $C$ be a crude stand-in for the context acquisition cost of a modification, and $M$ the realistic modification being attempted. CMP-style thinking asks how design rules reshape the cost of acquiring sufficient context for $M$—not whether $C$ is already an exact scalar.[^notation]

$$
\mathbb{E}[\text{mistakes} \mid C \text{ large}] \geq \mathbb{E}[\text{mistakes} \mid C \text{ small}]
$$

The inequality is deliberately cartoonish: it is a mnemonic, not a theorem. The book chapters will replace slogans with definitions, measurement hooks, and falsifiable predictions.
